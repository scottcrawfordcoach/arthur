Hi Scott,
Have you heard of EAT and NEAT with regard to metabolism?
EAT is Exercise Activity Thermogenesis - basically generating heat by expending energy
during intentional exercise. A run, a cycle, lifting weights etc.
NEAT is Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis - generating heat by expending energy during
incidental activities. This could be many things; fidgeting, walking to the bathroom, brushing
teeth etc.
Trying to burn calories to lose weight by exercising hard is not impossible, but it‚Äôs generally
considered a sub-optimal approach.
Strength training still has its place - muscle tissue burns more calories just to exist, the
mitochondria in muscle tissue is where much of the body‚Äôs energy is produced, and simply
being stronger and with better functional movement means that we‚Äôll have a higher NEAT
simply because it‚Äôs easier to move and thus, not such a chore to do things.

The body is aggressively homeostatic, meaning it will do everything it can to maintain the
current status quo.
One of the main ways it does this is by offsetting the energy demands of vigorous exercise by
down-regulating your NEAT.
You may fidget just a little less, lean on walls when standing, doze in a chair - any tiny little
thing the body can do to claw back those calories throughout the day. It may also keep you
just a little cooler, make you think a little slower, and drive up your appetite.
Since even an extremely athletic person might only burn up to 30% or so of daily calories
from deliberate exercise, NEAT makes up a bigger component of the total daily energy
expenditure.
This makes it a difficult system to overcome, but also a powerful lever we can pull.
When we are looking for sustainable long-term fat loss, it‚Äôs a case of clawing back just a few
tens of calories here and there without the body noticing - small exclusions from our diet and
lots of gentle aerobic exercise to burn fat, but not drive a big recovery response.
Low volume heavy lifts in the gym can be useful to help maintain muscle tissue without
excess fatigue.
Increase your NEAT with small adjustments to your routine; take the stairs, park farther from
the store, keep your shoes upstairs! I keep my toothbrush in the basement bathroom, so
every time I use it, I need to do 2 flights of stairs!
Don‚Äôt try to beat your body into submission by exhausting yourself - it almost always wins.
Get sneaky!
